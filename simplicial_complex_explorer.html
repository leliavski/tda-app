k-skeleton The sub-complex containing all simplices up to dimension k. The 0-skeleton is just the 5 points. The 1-skeleton adds edges when distance ≤ 2ε. The 2-skeleton further adds triangles. Each skeleton grows as ε increases.
Nerve of a cover Given any collection of sets that cover a space, build a simplicial complex where each set = one vertex, and add a simplex whenever a group of sets share at least one common point. The nerve of the ε-ball cover drawn above is the Čech complex — this connection is called the Nerve Lemma.
Čech complex C(ε) green fill Place a ball of radius ε around each point. Add a triangle for points A, B, C only if all three balls share a common intersection point. This is the topologically faithful construction — by the Nerve Lemma it is homotopy equivalent to the union of balls. However it can be slow to compute for high-dimensional data.
Vietoris-Rips complex VR(ε) orange fill = extra Simpler rule: add a triangle whenever all 3 pairwise distances ≤ 2ε (i.e., whenever all 3 edges exist). It is a "clique complex." Much easier to compute than Čech, but can include triangles whose balls do not share a common point. Try ε ≈ 77–86 above to see the difference on the equilateral triangle A–B–C.
Key insight: Čech ⊆ VR Every Čech simplex is also a VR simplex — but not vice versa. The equilateral triangle A–B–C (side ≈ 150) illustrates this: at ε = 75 all three edges appear (VR fills the triangle in orange), but the three balls only gain a common intersection at ε ≈ 87 (Čech turns it green). In high dimensions, the gap is even larger. They are always related by the sandwich C(ε) ⊆ VR(ε) ⊆ C(2ε).
